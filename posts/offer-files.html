Offer Files

How offer files turn a private swap into a plain text file you can paste anywhere - and let a whole ecosystem share one pool of liquidity that never lives inside any single app.

The throughput wall

Privacy comes with a computational cost - you have to generate proofs - and that computational cost comes with a throughput cost. Every zero-knowledge chain - Midnight included - trades raw transactions-per-second for the ability to compute privately. That squeezes an exchange from both sides. The classic order-book design puts every order on chain: placing an order is a transaction, cancelling it is another, and the large majority of resting orders are never filled at all - a lot of block space spent on intentions. The usual way out is an automated market maker, which keeps order flow off chain - but an AMM is a general-purpose smart contract, and on a privacy chain a general contract means a far heavier proof than simply placing an order. Either way, the naive exchange is expensive here.

The modern fix is the intent: you broadcast what you want off chain, and the chain is only touched when a solver actually fills it. Offer Files are this pattern - with a twist that makes them unusually clean on Midnight.

zSwap: swaps live in the L1

On most chains, swapping two assets means deploying a smart contract. Midnight builds atomic swaps directly into the base layer via a primitive called zSwap. The unit you hold is an offer, and an offer is a deliberately imbalanced transaction: it contributes some assets and withdraws others, and on its own it does not add up.

Concretely, every offer carries a signed amount per token - its deltas map. Alice's offer might read { NIGHT: +5, ROCK: −3 }: it contributes five NIGHT and withdraws three ROCK. The ledger only accepts a transaction whose every token nets to zero, so an offer alone is never valid. It is one half of a trade, waiting for the other half.

Two offers merge by unioning their inputs and outputs and summing their deltas element-wise. Crucially, each offer already embeds its own zero-knowledge proof, so combining them re-proves nothing: the taker needs no proving system, and the merge is fully non-interactive. Complementary halves cancel to a balanced whole that the chain accepts in one shot.

It is tamper-proof by construction. Altering any byte of an offer breaks the embedded proof, rendering it unusable, and the homomorphic commitments binding the pieces together mean no one can strip your coins out for a different trade. You can hand an Offer File to a stranger; the worst they can do is discard it.

zSwap vs. a ZswapOffer

A quick naming aside, because the Midnight codebase is precise about it and the distinction matters here. zSwap (lowercase z) is the whole system - the shielded, private token machinery, UTXO-like but backed by commitment and nullifier sets you can never directly enumerate. A ZswapOffer is one concrete structure inside the Midnight codebase: a bundle of inputs (coins spent), outputs (coins created), transients, and a deltas map. It's the unit that gets merged, balanced, and ultimately submitted as part of a transaction.

The privacy model is stronger than people expect. At the level of an individual coin, zSwap hides everything: there are no addresses (ownership is proven in zero-knowledge), and even the asset type and amount are sealed away inside cryptographic commitments. The only party who can read what an output actually holds is its receiver, through a note encrypted just for them. So how does anyone learn what's being traded? One thing is public, by necessity: the offer's deltas - its net imbalance per token ({ NIGHT: +5, ROCK: −3 }), declared in the clear. The chain must see it to confirm the offer balances, so it can't be hidden.

Technical aside - how a value can stay hidden yet still be checkable (most readers can skip this)

The commitments that hide each coin's value are a form of semi-homomorphic encryption: you can't read the hidden value, but you can add two commitments together and get a valid commitment to the sum. That is the whole reason the chain can confirm an offer balances - add up the committed inputs, subtract the outputs and the public deltas, and check that the result cancels to zero - without ever seeing a single amount.

Pedersen commitments are one common implementation of this (typically over an elliptic curve, as Midnight uses): a value becomes a curve point, blinded by a random factor so it reveals nothing, yet two such points still add. It's worth flagging because that additive structure is exactly what the L2's recursive proof later leans on - it lets the prover fold and aggregate these commitments across every swap, rather than re-deriving balances from scratch.

From a swap to a file

If an offer is a self-contained, tamper-proof, mergeable object, why keep it on chain? You don't. The Offer Files MIP serializes a proven offer and encodes it with bech32m, producing a plain ASCII string. That string is the Offer File. It carries a checksum so a copy-paste error can't silently corrupt it.

And here's why that string is safe to share: an offer's trade terms live in its public deltas, while the coins and the parties stay private. So an Offer File reveals what's on offer - "gives 5 NIGHT, wants 3 ROCK" - and nothing else. You can post it in the open and let anyone match it, without leaking who you are or what else you hold. Open trade terms, private everything else - that's the property the rest of this system is built on.

It is not small - the proofs dominate, so a minimal offer is roughly 16,000 characters (too large for a standard QR code). But it is just text: zswapoffer1qpzry9x8gf2tvdw0s3jn54khce6mua7l… You can paste it into a chat, attach it as a file, or host it behind a URL. No complex setup rquired.

An Offer File stays valid until one of two things happens: the maker spends the coins it was built from, or its time-to-live expires. Both are judged against current chain state - which turns out to be exactly half of the indexing problem we hit later.

Where do offers live?

You can absolutely keep an offer file private and hand it straight to someone - and that alone is useful. Think back to NFT mania, when people traded by posting in Discord: that needed a trusted escrow, because either side could renege. An offer file gives you that same casual peer-to-peer trade with no escrow and more privacy - paste it to a friend and the swap either settles atomically or not at all.

But peer-to-peer means only the people you send it to can see it. The bigger prize is shared liquidity: making your offer visible to as many takers as possible. For that our reference design uses Celestia as a public bulletin board - a data-availability layer whose only job is to accept a blob of data and guarantee everyone can download it. It never executes the data - there are no smart contracts here - it merely makes it available.

The killer feature is namespaces. Block data is partitioned into per-application lanes via a namespaced Merkle tree, so a client can sync only the Offer-File namespace rather than the whole chain - and, thanks to the tree's range labelling, still verify that it received every offer in that lane, with none omitted. Combined with data availability sampling, a light node gets close to full-node assurance that the offers are real and complete.

One honest caveat: data availability guarantees the blob was published, not that it is stored forever. Light clients retain roughly a 7-day window - but that isn't a real limitation here, because Midnight's L1 enforces a global two-week time-to-live on offers anyway, so a live offer never outlives the window. Durable history of old offers is something the application arranges itself (a pinned namespace, snapshots, or a paid archive).

Two genuinely hard parts

The theory is clean; two practical problems are not, and the reference implementation exists mainly to solve them.

1. You must watch two chains at once

A correct order book needs both halves of the story. Celestia tells you which offers were created. Midnight tells you which are dead: the instant any coin an offer was built from is spent, that offer can never be filled. So you index Celestia for new offers and Midnight for the spends that invalidate them. The reference build uses EffectStream, a framework that lets one program consume both chains as a single ordered stream - new-offer events from Celestia, spent-output events from Midnight - reconciling them into one live book. (The full worked example is its zSwap-DA template.)

2. Users don't hold Celestia's token

Posting a blob to Celestia costs a fee paid in TIA. But a Midnight user holds Midnight assets - expecting them to acquire TIA just to advertise an offer is a non-starter. So EffectStream ships a batcher that stands in the middle: the user hands it the offer, and the batcher submits the blob and pays the TIA fee on their behalf. The user never has to know Celestia is involved.

The payoff: shared liquidity

Put it together and the trades stop living inside any one application. Every DEX and every NFT marketplace built this way reads from the same pool of Offer Files. An app reduces to two things: a filter over the shared offers and a frontend to present them. Liquidity one site attracts is liquidity every other site can match against - the network effect is no longer captured by a single venue.

That's the technical payoff - one shared pool of liquidity that no single venue owns. But shipping a clean architecture is not the same as earning real trading: “we can build it” and “people will use it” are different claims. The second one deserves an honest answer rather than a hand-wave, and - unusually for a new design - there's a real-world experiment to answer it with. So we return to the business case at the very end, once the design is complete.

Now build a DEX with it

An order book almost falls out of offer files for free: each file is a resting order, and a marketplace is just a filter over them. For a taker it's ideal - browse, pick one, settle with a single on-chain transaction; listing and re-listing orders costs nothing. The catch hides in how Midnight represents a swap. An offer is built from a specific coin - a UTXO (unspent transaction output) - and the offer is valid only while that coin stays unspent.

So the lifecycle is lopsided. Creating and sharing an offer is free and off-chain. A fill is one on-chain transaction (settlement spends the coin). But a cancel is also an on-chain transaction - and that is fundamental, not a missing optimization. Once copies of your offer are loose in the world, the only way to make every one un-fillable is to spend the coin they all reference. There is no off-chain “unsend”: on a UTXO chain, cancelling is spending, and spending is a transaction.

The market-maker wall

A casual order book is one thing; a good one needs market makers - participants who quote a buy price and a sell price at once and earn the small spread between them. When two offers cross (someone selling a touch cheap, someone buying a touch high), a market maker fills both and pockets the difference. Repeat that continuously and you get tight prices and deep liquidity - the entire reason exchanges court market makers.

But quoting is never set-and-forget. The fair price moves constantly, and the moment it does, a maker's whole ladder is mispriced and must be cancelled and re-posted. Professional makers cancel far more orders than they ever fill - cancel-to-fill ratios above 90% are routine. On Midnight every one of those cancels is an on-chain transaction, and a ZK L1 clears only a handful per second with slow finality. A maker who needs to re-quote dozens of times a second simply can't - so serious market making is, quietly, impossible.

And that is the missing piece. Offer files give us a shared, off-chain order book and real settlement on the base layer - but for it to be a venue market makers can actually work, cancellation has to be fast and nearly free. The fix is a layer-2 (L2) built specifically for offer files. So let's design it.

Designing the L2: integrity vs privacy

Building an L2 on a privacy chain forces two questions to the front. First, integrity: under what assumptions can someone prove they hold a balance on the L2 and withdraw it back to the L1? Second, privacy: who, if anyone, may see the L2's state? On Midnight privacy isn't a feature you bolt on - it's the premise - so both questions are in play simultaneously, and they pull against each other.

There's a useful reference point. Another Midnight project, Midnight City, already shipped an L2: they use the Ligero proof system for privacy, the Sovereign SDK to build the chain, and for integrity they run the sequencer inside a TEE (a trusted execution environment - a secure hardware enclave) that settles to a Midnight L1 contract. The L1 trusts the enclave's attestation that the L2 transitioned correctly.

That's a sound design - but ours is financial, and for money we don't want to rest withdrawals on hardware trust. A TEE is one disclosed side-channel away from forged exits. We want cryptographic integrity: a proof, not a promise from a chip. And on a private chain, that is genuinely hard - for a reason that comes straight out of how zSwap works.

The hard part: proving a private balance

On a zSwap chain, balances are private: they live as commitments and nullifiers that nobody can attribute to an owner just by looking. So when you go to withdraw, the L2 can't vouch for your balance - the sequencer genuinely doesn't know it. The proof has to come from you.

Midnight gives you the tool to start: a viewing key, which lets you (and only you) detect which swaps involved your address. But detecting your swaps isn't the same as proving your balance. To prove it, you must show - for every other swap as well - that it is not yours; otherwise you could understate deposits or invent them. Your proof therefore has to sweep the L2's entire history, not just your handful of swaps.

Even a modest L2 produces more swaps in a day than a user could ever fold into one proof - recursive SNARKs and all. You'd be able to prove only the last few minutes of history before the work explodes; the chain, meanwhile, runs forever. At any real throughput, the naive approach is simply dead.

So here's the corner: cryptographic integrity demands a proof, privacy means only you can build it, and building it naively means re-proving the entire chain for every user. It looks like a dead end - but there's a clever way out.

One proof for everyone

The trick is to stop making users prove their own balances. Have the sequencer prove once, for everyone at the same time. The catch: to compute everyone's balance the prover needs everyone's viewing key - exactly what must stay private. So the trusted enclave returns, in a far smaller role: each user sends their viewing key into the sequencer's TEE, where the keys can be used but the operator can never read them.

Inside the enclave, that one recursive SNARK can read every balance, so it folds the entire history exactly once into a single proof covering all users - the whole-history scan that was hopeless per-user now happens once for everyone. And because the proof is succinct, it reveals none of those balances: it only commits to them, and each user can later open their own slice of that commitment to withdraw, without re-proving anything.

And here is the elegant part - the trust split. The enclave is not vouching for the money; the proof does that, cryptographically, and is unforgeable even if the hardware fails. The TEE's only job is to keep the viewing keys private. So where Midnight City's design trusts hardware for integrity (a breach forges funds), here a broken enclave costs you privacy, never funds. Integrity is math; the hardware is only guarding the curtain.

So we have integrity and privacy: one proof in an enclave settles the whole L2 back to Midnight, cryptographically and without revealing a balance. That same proof does one more job worth pausing on - it's also how you get your funds back out.

Getting your funds back out

One question remains: once your funds live on the L2, how do you get them out, back to the L1? On the L2 you have more than create offer and cancel offer - there's a third move, offboard. An offboard is an unbalanced swap that locks the funds you want to withdraw and makes them unspendable on the L2. That imbalance - funds leaving with nothing coming back - is your exit amount.

Because the offboard is part of the L2 state, it rides inside the same recursive proof the sequencer posts to the L1. The L1 contract reads the proof, sees that this address offboarded this much, and completes the other half by paying the funds to the same address on the L1. Same address on both sides is the entire security: only the address that locked the funds can claim them, so no one can intercept your exit.

Step back and it's an offer file again - just split across two layers. One half lives on the L2 (lock and declare), the other on the L1 (pay out), bound together by a shared address. The pattern that opened this talk is also how you leave it. (Onboarding is simply the mirror image: lock on the L1, credit the same address on the L2.) With the exit covered, the core design is complete - and what's left is making it fast.

Scaling, part 1: the TEE off the hot path

Reassuringly, the enclave isn't in the way of anyone using the system. Recall that an offer file is transparent about the assets and amounts in a swap - it hides only the addresses. So the everyday path needs no enclave at all: a user hands their offer file to an ordinary sequencer, which orders it and surfaces it on explorers and order books right away. No special hardware on the path every user touches.

The TEE sits off to the side as a separate system: every so often it pulls the latest swaps from the indexer, decrypts them with the viewing keys, runs the single recursive proof, and posts it to the L1. It's the prover, not the gatekeeper - nothing waits on it. Keeping it off the hot path is a choice, not a requirement (you could run the whole sequencer inside a TEE), but the less code that must be enclave-compatible, the simpler the system is to build and audit - so we keep the trusted surface small.

It's worth pausing on what this design keeps public, because it's the quiet reason a zSwap DEX can actually work. Offer files reveal the assets and amounts and hide only the addresses - so a marketplace can show real volume, liquidity, and prices while never exposing who traded. That middle ground is deliberate. Earlier “fully private” DEXs hid the amounts too, and they've struggled to find a market for three compounding reasons: traders actively want to see how much liquidity is available and what the current price is, so total privacy works against the very people it's meant to serve; hiding amounts usually forces you into MPC or FHE machinery, a steep jump in complexity and cost; and producing trustworthy liquidity statistics - the thing users actually want - without leaking the private state is genuinely hard to do cryptographically. Public amounts with hidden addresses sidesteps all three: the stats fall out for free, and the only thing guarded is identity.

Scaling, part 2: consensus without trust

Start with consensus - how the L2 orders transactions and finalizes them. What makes this easy: because security is cryptographic (it's a ZK rollup - the proof guarantees correctness), consensus doesn't have to provide security at all. It only has to order transactions and agree they're final. A misbehaving validator can stall or reorder, but it can never forge state - the proof catches that. So we're free to choose consensus purely for performance and for keeping MEV low.

Three reasons a BFT system is the right fit here:

  1. Security is already cryptographic, so consensus is free to optimize for something other than safety.
  2. We care about performance, and BFT gives fast, deterministic finality by design - no waiting out probabilistic confirmations.
  3. We want to minimize MEV, and a known committee with threshold signing is a good foundation to build that on - MEV-mitigation schemes like fair ordering or encrypted mempools could be layered on top, though that's significant extra engineering, not something the consensus gives you for free.

Concretely you can sit anywhere on the spectrum above: a single sequencer to start, round-robin to decentralize cheaply, or a protocol like Simplex where a BLS threshold signature lets the L1 - and anyone - verify the agreed L2 state from a single small signature.

Scaling, part 3: abstracting away the data layer

The original Offer File proposal posts the files to Celestia, and everyone reads them from its namespace. That's a reasonable starting point, but it puts two frictions right in front of users. Celestia today has roughly 6-second blocks, but with instant finality - decent for a live order book. However, it drags a foreign token into the experience: to post an offer you need Celestia's TIA, or you lean on a batcher to pay it for you. Either way, the data layer is something users and dApp developers have to reason about.

Our L2 makes that whole concern disappear - the data layer drops out of view for everyone except the sequencers. Here's the shape of it:

Once the L2 exists, this all dissolves. Tools sync the L2 state directly, and by construction that state already contains every offer and every fill - so there's no Celestia namespace to scan and no DA token in a user's wallet. The L2 could still use Celestia under the hood for availability, or swap in another DA layer, or simply post its data to Midnight itself. That's now an implementation detail the sequencers own, not something users or developers ever touch - and the speed worry moves to the L2's own fast finality from part 2, which we control.

Open questions

That's the core design end to end - but it would be dishonest to stop without the problems we haven't solved. Three of them are worth stating plainly.

#1 - the nullifier set never shrinks

Most zero-knowledge systems, Midnight included, prevent double-spends with nullifiers. On a transparent UTXO chain you'd simply mark a spent output as spent. zSwap wants the same guarantee - each UTXO spent at most once - but without revealing which UTXO it was. So rather than marking the UTXO directly, spending it publishes a nullifier: a deterministic, private tag derived from that UTXO that reveals nothing about it. The ledger keeps the set of nullifiers - effectively the set of spent UTXOs - and rejects any nullifier it has already seen. The same UTXO always yields the same nullifier, so it can never be spent twice, and no observer can tell which UTXO a nullifier came from.

On the base layer that's tolerable. But the entire point of the L2 is high throughput - and every swap adds a nullifier that is never removed (commitment roots expire; nullifiers don't). At real volumes the set grows into the billions, and every proof has to be made against it. Keeping that state from blowing up, without surrendering the privacy nullifiers buy us, is a genuinely open problem.

#2 - pricing and ordering transactions

How should the L2 order transactions, and what should they cost? Fees, first-come, an auction? It sounds mundane, but three forces pull against each other: (1) MEV & ordering - with no fees you tend to get off-chain backroom deals (quietly paying a block producer to jump the queue); a trusted BFT committee mitigates this by design, but only because you're trusting it. (2) Market-maker economics - real exchanges charge makers and takers differently precisely so makers aren't punished, and makers cancel and replace constantly, so any meaningful per-tx fee bleeds them. (3) Spam - set fees to zero and you invite junk transactions that slow proofs and (per #1) balloon the nullifier set permanently.

The best the figure can do - a low flat fee with first-come ordering - is the closest thing to an answer, and it still isn't clean. First-come ordering only stops bidding for position on-chain; off-chain, you're back to trusting the committee not to take a reorder bribe. And a single flat fee can't do what exchanges do with separate maker and taker fees - charge the spammer and the taker without taxing every maker re-quote. How fees and ordering should work on this L2 is still open.

#3 - how much to build, and when

The last question isn't really technical - it's about timing. How much complexity do we want? A perfect system takes longer to build, and this whole L2 has a shelf life, because two things could make it unnecessary:

  1. The Midnight L1 itself. It keeps improving, and it runs general-purpose ZK-optimized smart contracts - not just zSwaps - so if the base layer gets fast enough, you don't need this L2 at all.
  2. Nightstream, a more general Midnight L2 built as a full zkVM rather than a ZK-optimized construction; as it matures, the case for a zSwap-specific network shrinks.

But neither has arrived: it's unclear how long until the L1 matches a purpose-built zSwap L2, and Nightstream today has no clear path to connect to the L1 and is far slower than this L2 would be.

That points to a clear, if uncomfortable, answer: don't aim for perfect. With an open-ended window, the cost of over-building isn't just late delivery - it's shipping into a world that no longer needs what you built. Better to get something good to market soon, while the window is open, and iterate from there. Which is as honest a note as any to end on: a design that works, and the real questions still worth arguing about.

Switching hats: would anyone actually trade here?

Everything above is engineering - how to make offer files work, and work fast, on a privacy chain. That is the easy half to feel good about, and the dangerous half to stop at: a protocol that is elegant and unused is just a clever way to spend a budget. So switch hats - from architect to investor - and ask the only question that decides whether any of this was worth building: will people actually trade on it? We can't prove the future, but we aren't guessing in the dark either, because this exact shape already ran as a live experiment for four and a half years.

The one thing we don't have to guess: Chia ran it

The dexie marketplace on the Chia blockchain shipped a conceptually identical offer-file system - wallet-created offers, a peer-to-peer gossip network, and shared liquidity across every site that indexed it. It is the closest thing the industry has to a controlled experiment on the idea, and the headline result is genuinely reassuring: it worked. Across 4.5 years and multiple boom-and-bust waves, dexie settled on the order of ~985,000 real, on-chain trades carried entirely by offer files. We pulled and analyzed 833,145 of them - the full breakdown is its own post, Chia Offer Files by the Numbers, which the rest of this section leans on. Offer files are not a whiteboard idea: they clear trades - though, as the next figures show, not many dollars of them.

Read those numbers the other way and they are sobering - and because a single dollar figure would mislead on a chain whose token fell from ~$30 to under $3, the honest, non-cherry-picked number is coin-denominated: the median trade held at about 0.2 XCH across the whole period (NFT prices were famously sticky at 0.1–0.2 XCH the entire time). In dollars the typical swap collapsed from ~$34 in 2022 to under a dollar by 2026 - as XCH fell ~15× and sub-cent game-token swaps flooded in - so it's a couple of dollars at most, small by any reading. The totals agree: ~1.08M XCH has ever changed hands, which - pricing each swap at the true rate on its day - is only about $16.9M of lifetime volume (a floor); the busiest liquid pair clears ~$800/day across ~17 trades; and nearly 90% of offers are a plain one-for-one swap. After 4.5 years, the honest description is a retail bazaar - real, durable, and small. The optimist's footnote is fair: scale is a property of the ecosystem, not the primitive, and Chia is a small chain in a long bear market. The pessimist's reply is equally fair: Midnight is a young ecosystem too, so there's no reason to assume it clears more out of the gate.

What it proved the primitive does well

Several of the strongest pro-offer-file claims survive contact with four years of data, and they carry over to Midnight because they are properties of the offer itself, not of Chia:

  • Trustless, atomic settlement. The trade completes in full or does nothing - no escrow, no counterparty risk.
  • Atomic multi-asset bundles. ~10% of offers carried three or more assets, with a tail to a single 32-asset trade - baskets an instrument-at-a-time order book can't express.
  • Safe composition into an aggregator. Because every route is one atomic spend, by 2026 roughly half of all dexie offers were multi-source routes; the worst case of routing through an untrusted source is simply that nothing happens (that same composability, we'll see, has a dark side).
  • A free price oracle. The settled-price tape reconstructs XCH/USD with a ~1.8% median spread, stable on as few as five trades a day.

One further result is striking but - fair warning - not portable the way those are: on Chia, trustless trading strengthened NFT royalties rather than eroding them (the trade-weighted median rose from 5% to 10%). That rides on Chia's NFT puzzle standard, not on the offer file itself, so whether Midnight's contract model reproduces it is an open question, not a carried-over win.

What it warns about

The warnings are just as concrete, and they are about the market, not the mechanism. Given total freedom, the offer-file market converged on the architecture it was built to avoid. By 2026 roughly two-thirds of fills were taken by a single automated market maker (TibetSwap - 46% rising toward 67% of labeled fills), and discovery re-centralized on one aggregator: dexie routes about half of all offers - the flip side of the composability we just praised, the same force funnelling flow into a single venue. So much for the opening promise of liquidity shared permissionlessly across apps: the primitive stayed decentralized, but the market it produced did not. Worse for anyone hoping to provide liquidity, a resting offer is a free option you write to arbitrage bots. An offer priced in the taker's favor fills in a median ~38 seconds; a fairly-priced one waits ~10 minutes - textbook adverse selection. So makers couldn't earn a spread by sitting still; they had to cancel and re-quote constantly, producing eight to nine wasted offers for every one that settled (2.2M reward-earning offers in ten months, 99.7% of which never filled). And because none of that paid on spread, liquidity had to be rented with a token subsidy - which then shrank ~5×, from ~$1,700/month to ~$355, as the reward token itself fell. Whether that means the market was weaning off rented liquidity or the subsidy was simply eroding with its token, the data can't say - but liquidity still leaning on a subsidy years in was, at best, not yet organic.

Now add Midnight

Here is where our specific chain changes the calculus - in both directions. The points above were argued chain-agnostically; Midnight is not neutral about any of them.

Midnight's biggest lever lands on the bear case's strongest pillar - but it's a lever, not a cure. The maker-killer on Chia was that cancelling is an on-chain spend, so makers churned millions of quotes and still got picked off. The L2 in the back half of this post makes cancelling fast and nearly free, which removes the cost of churning. What it doesn't remove is the cause: a stale quote still gets picked off in the window before you can pull it, and on Midnight every fresh quote has to be proven - so proving, not cancelling, may be the step that matters more, unless proofs can be made cheap or amortised across quotes. So the L2 makes market-making cheaper to attempt, not automatically profitable; whether makers come out ahead still rides on the L2's unsolved fee and ordering design and on how fast proofs can be produced. What does carry over cleanly is the rest: zSwap gives atomic settlement and multi-asset bundles in the base layer, and Midnight adds one thing Chia simply cannot - real privacy, hidden traders over public prices.

But Midnight also imports a cost Chia never paid. On Chia the whole DEX ran on about half a percent of block compute; an offer is cheap to verify. On Midnight the proof is still an expensive part - a single offer is ~16,000 characters of zero-knowledge proof, and settling a trade means a taker merges and posts that proof on-chain to be verified, with the matching overhead that implies. The L1 clears only a handful of transactions a second, and the nullifier set grows without bound. Those are real costs, and they cap throughput. But notice the shape of the bet, not just the price tag: in ZK the hard part is rarely the idea - it's finding one cheap enough to run today - and an offer file is about as light as a useful primitive gets. That it consumed a fraction of Chia's compute is actually encouraging here, because it's exactly the kind of lean primitive zSwap can afford under ZK now, where a general-purpose contract still can't. So the cost is genuine, but it's also why this primitive - and not a richer one - is the buildable bet. What stays unresolved is narrower: the fees and ordering questions still bear directly on whether makers can work, the economics that broke on Chia.

Then there's the AMM - the relationship most people read exactly backwards. Chia's market became an AMM: pooled liquidity, not resting offers, is what ultimately scaled. That invites the sharpest bear question: why build offer files if the market just turns into a pool you could have shipped on day one? But look at how it happened on Chia: the AMM didn't replace offer files, it plugged into them - TibetSwap fills offers and settles through the very same rail. Read that way, “the market became an AMM” isn't offer files losing; it's offer files succeeding as the substrate a pool runs on. And that's the likelier shape on Midnight too. Building a general-purpose AMM here means a general-purpose ZK contract - hard and slow, at least today - so offer files give you a working market now and let pools arrive gradually, as ZK makes them feasible, with the decentralized offer chain always underneath as the fallback rather than a cliff to transition off. Better still, because a new pool can bootstrap its liquidity straight from the shared offer-file pool, this path may even soften the winner-take-all gravity of AMMs - later entrants don't start from zero. The string attached: if pools never get cheap enough, offer files must carry liquidity alone, and Chia's verdict on resting orders still applies. So - a genuinely promising complementarity, with one real caveat, not a dead end.

Underneath all of this sits the question neither chain's data answers: who is this for? “Public prices, hidden traders” is not a universal want; it's a specific one. The plausible buyer is flow that must prove an execution price but not reveal who traded - OTC desks, treasuries rebalancing without being front-run, anyone allergic to MEV. That's a real niche, but it is emphatically not Chia's ~$2-a-swap retail NFT trader - which is the deeper reason to be careful extrapolating Chia at all: the mechanism may port while the user does not. But that gap cuts the other way too. Ask why the bigger players never showed up on Chia. Part of it may be that serious market-making is simply hard to incentivize at size - but part may be Chia's transparency itself, the very thing Midnight removes. If privacy is what kept large, identity-sensitive flow away from a fully public venue, then it isn't just a different user, it's a larger addressable market - one Chia structurally couldn't reach. Whether that demand actually shows up - far more than any throughput number - is the real, still-unmeasured question underneath all of this.

The honest verdict

Put the optimist's case in one breath: the shape is proven to clear real trades for years; Midnight's L2 directly attacks the defect that crippled market-making on Chia by making cancels cheap; privacy - public prices, hidden traders - is a real differentiator no transparent DEX has; and while general-purpose AMMs stay hard to build here, offer files may be the natural first liquidity primitive rather than a detour. And the Chia→AMM convergence may simply not bind here, because an AMM would struggle to offer hidden traders over public prices - so for the buyer who wants exactly that, there's little pool to lose to. If that reading holds, the move is the one the time-to-market question already pointed to - ship something lean and capture the window before it (or the moat) closes.

Now the skeptic's case, equally honest: the proven shape proved small - an ~$2-median retail bazaar - and it converged on an AMM and a centralized aggregator regardless of how trustless the rail underneath was. Chia's data is steady-state and silent on the hardest part, the cold start - and Chia still had to rent liquidity with a subsidy, so a younger Midnight, where every fresh quote also costs a proof, plausibly has to bootstrap harder, not easier. The maker economics hang on an L2 whose fee, ordering, and nullifier questions are open. And the pick-off that cheap cancels don't fully remove already has a rival answer shipping elsewhere - batch-auction privacy DEXs like Penumbra sidestep it by clearing in sealed batches instead of letting quotes rest at all. (That a competitor exists cuts both ways: it's a sharper rival, but also a sign the demand for private trading is real.) By this reading the bar for traction is higher on Midnight than it was on Chia, not lower.

Both readings are built from the same receipts, and that is the point. The evidence is strong enough to justify building something, and honest enough that nobody should pretend the traction is proven. What it really turns on is a demand no one has measured yet: whether enough people want a market that is transparent in its prices and private in its participants to bring real liquidity to it. The architecture, we've shown, can be built. Whether it should be is a bet - but now, at least, it's a bet you make with four and a half years of someone else's data in hand.
