Chia Offer Files by the Numbers

830,000 completed trades, four and a half years of Chia offer-file activity. Here is what people actually did with them — and why the data tells a different story than the pitch.

The data, and what to trust

Every record here is one completed Chia offer — a trade that actually settled on-chain — as indexed by the dexie.space aggregator. (Chia's native coin is XCH, the ticker that recurs throughout.) The snapshot runs from 2022-01-14 to 2026-05-23 and holds 833,145 unique settled offers.

One caveat, applied throughout. This is ~84.7% of all offers ever settled, and the gap is not random. dexie's API caps every trading pair at its newest 10,000 records, so the oldest history of the busiest pairs (anything against XCH or a stablecoin) is clipped. Recent activity and overall shape are reliable; early absolute counts for hot pairs are a floor, not an exact figure. Every number below was computed from the local dump with a committed query — no estimates.

Most offer files never fill

One more thing those 833,145 records are: settled offers — trades that actually filled. We use only those, because money only moves on a fill. But settling is the exception: most offer files sit open, expire unfilled, or get cancelled. Putting a number on the rest is hard, because dexie prunes cancelled and expired offers from its records — so we estimate that bucket from market-maker reward data (the program pays makers to post offers, 99.7% of which never settle; full justification in the incentive section later). The rough picture: of every offer file ever created, only about 1 in 8 settles; the rest — on the order of millions — are cancelled or expired. So treat the chart below as estimates, and everything else in this report as being about that ~1-in-8 minority that actually traded.

The shape and scale of the market

Step back and the market is unglamorous in a telling way. Nearly 90% of offers are a single asset for a single asset, the median trade is 0.2 XCH (about $2.70), and 76.5% of all offers touch XCH — it is the hub the whole market trades against. And the totals confirm the flea-market read: about 1.08 million XCH has changed hands across every settled offer, which — valuing each swap at the true XCH price on the day it settled — works out to roughly $16.9 million of lifetime volume (detailed in Part III). This is a retail bazaar measured in tens of millions of dollars, not an institutional venue.

But a real minority goes the other way and exploits something offer files are uniquely good at: bundling many assets into one all-or-nothing trade. About 10% of offers carry 3+ assets, 2.7% carry 4+, and 2.2% carry 5+ — NFT bundles, liquidity-pool deposits (give XCH + a token, get an LP token), and the like. The tail runs surprisingly far: a single atomic offer can pack up to 32 assets, with oddly common bundle sizes at 5 and 11.

Brutally concentrated, with a graveyard at the end

Only 860 distinct fungible tokens (CATs) have ever traded — versus a cloud of 288,945 distinct NFTs. And among those 860, the top 5% carry roughly 73% of all trades while the bottom 80% account for under 6%. 176 tokens traded five times or fewer; 84 traded exactly once.

The killer app: NFTs

NFTs are the biggest non-XCH story on offer files. 38.1% of all offers involve an NFT, and NFT → XCH is the #1 trade route in the entire dataset at 224,879 offers — bigger than any fungible pair. The direction is overwhelmingly sell-side (~99%): the offer file is a marketplace listing — "here is my NFT, pay me X."

A sticky XCH floor — priced in coin, not dollars

The median NFT has sold for a remarkably sticky 0.1–0.3 XCH across the whole four years — traders anchor to round XCH amounts, not to dollars. Because the floor is XCH-denominated, the dollar value of a typical NFT just rode XCH's own price down. Pricing each sale against the true XCH/USD rate that day (an outside exchange feed, not the in-house oracle — so it works for the full timeline, not just the warp.green era), the median NFT cleared for roughly $6–8 in 2022–23, slid to ~$2–4 in 2024–25, and now changes hands for around a dollar — even as the coin-denominated floor barely moved. The takeaway: the “floor” is a floor in XCH; in dollars it's pocket change, and it shrank ~6× because XCH itself fell ~15×.

NFT activity is moderately concentrated — the top 10 collections are 54% of all XCH volume. By trade count the leaders are a viral 2025 wave (go4.me), a farming game's lands and characters (FarmerVerse), and the classic ~10,000-piece generative profile-picture sets (ChiaPhunks, Chunks, Punk Friends):

But count isn't value. By XCH volume the leader is the blue-chip Chia Friends (59,100 XCH from 5,344 sales, median 7.5 XCH each) — the opposite profile from go4.me's ~30,000 trades at a ~0.07-XCH median. Two completely different shapes of "popular."

The trustless twist: do creators get paid?

Trustless P2P trading raises an uncomfortable question for NFT creators: with no marketplace in the middle, what stops a buyer from routing around the creator royalty? On most chains a royalty is a marketplace convention a direct trade can simply ignore. On Chia it is different — the royalty is baked into the NFT's on-chain puzzle (the NFT1 standard), so the settlement spend itself must satisfy it. The data says it holds: 100% of traded NFTs carry a royalty, only 7.6% are set to zero, and the trade-weighted median royalty has risen from 5% (2022–24) to 7% (2025) to 10% (2026) — creators raising their take in a market with nothing forcing buyers to pay, which only makes sense if the protocol itself honors it.

Behind those royalties is a small, identifiable cast: 99.96% of traded NFTs were minted by a creator with an on-chain DID — a persistent identity, which is what makes a durable royalty claim meaningful — and just 891 creators account for all NFT trading.

Whole game economies run on offer files

That game-economy bucket — ~20% of all offers, the second-largest token category after memecoins — is the real surprise: entire games clear their internal economies through offer files. The biggest is FarmerVerse (76,000 offers), a full production chain rendered as tokens: a currency (Farmer Bucks) that buys labor (Farmer- and Tractor-Hours, fuel), which yields crops (seed, hay), which raise animals (XCOW, XPIG, XSHEEP), which produce goods (milk, meat, wool, eggs) — every step a CAT swapped peer-to-peer. Abandoned Land (48,000 offers) is the same idea — players swap Wood for Ore for Food nearly 400,000 times and barely cash out to XCH (only ~8,500 times): an in-game crafting economy settling on a public blockchain instead of a game server. These aren't fringe novelties; collectively they out-trade the entire stablecoin segment.

Part IIWho's on the other side

The market is mostly machines

Offer files are pitched as trustless peer-to-peer trades. So who is actually on the other side? Since dexie began labeling fills (April 2025), 46% of all settled offers were taken not by a person but by a single automated market maker, TibetSwap — a bot quoting against a liquidity pool — rising toward two-thirds of all fills by 2026 (a floor: only this one AMM is labeled).

And automation isn't a recent arrival. Apply a simple bot fingerprint (fills in seconds, with pool-derived non-round amounts) across the whole timeline and you find automated-looking trades were already 36–51% of token swaps in 2023–24 — years before the TibetSwap label existed. The machines were always here; we just couldn't put a name on them.

The mechanics confirm it. An AMM fill settles in a median of ~35 seconds (70.7% sub-minute); a human peer-to-peer offer sits for a median of ~3.9 hours, sometimes months. For scale, Chia adds a transaction block only about every ~50 seconds — and its finality is probabilistic, firming up over a few minutes — so a ~35-second median means these fills land within roughly one block of an offer being posted, about as fast as the chain itself can settle. Humans pick round amounts (48.8% are multiples of 0.05 XCH); the AMM emits whatever the pool math returns. (There's even a P2P secondary market in the AMM's own liquidity-pool tokens, in 4.5% of all offers.)

You might expect all that automation to make prices tighter and more efficient. Surprisingly, it doesn't. Comparing the same pair in the same month (to strip out price drift), the AMM's fills are no tighter than human ones — if anything slightly wider (relative dispersion 0.149 vs 0.127). The mechanism is almost philosophical: humans cluster on round-number prices, which looks orderly, while the AMM faithfully quotes every micro-move of the curve. (As Part III shows, the bots enforce tightness a different way — by grabbing anything mispriced in seconds.)

How the AMM won: dexie became an aggregator

The bots didn't win by users flocking to a separate AMM site. dexie itself became a liquidity aggregator: its one-click Combined Swap routes your order through whatever prices it best — combined offers plus the TibetSwap pool — and settles the whole route atomically inside a single offer file (so a bad hop can't cost you anything; worst case the offer just doesn't complete). You can watch the aggregator take over: settlements that combine multiple sources go from zero before 2024 to roughly half of all offers by 2026. And it leans on the AMM: 59% of TibetSwap fills are these routed Combined Swaps. So "the AMM is half the market" is, to a large degree, "dexie routes orders to the AMM" — the offer file quietly became the settlement rail for an aggregator.

So who is actually trading?

It's the obvious question, and our data can't answer it — but not because the chain is private. Chia is public: every offer settles on-chain, and the maker's and taker's addresses are right there in the settlement spend. We just don't have them — the offer records we indexed expose the coins involved but not the puzzle hashes (addresses) behind them, and we didn't crawl the chain to resolve them. So "how many distinct traders" is answerable in principle; it's a gap in our indexing, not a property of the blockchain — and a clear next step. What we can already see is concentration: a single AMM contract is the counterparty to ~1 in 5 of all offers (46% of recent ones); just 891 creators mint all the NFTs; and automated trading was already a third to half of token swaps before any of it was labeled. So 833,145 trades is certainly not 833,145 people — it's a big, busy marketplace run by a surprisingly small cast of humans and bots.

There is one group we can put a hard number on — the market makers, whose addresses the reward data exposes — and it's a tiny, concentrated cast. But they get their own section later (the AMM and who earns the rewards), so we won't double up here. Instead, a different lens on "who": when they trade.

When they trade — and roughly where

We can't see traders' locations, but we can see their clocks. Posting times trace a clear daily rhythm: human NFT trading roughly doubles from a ~04:00 UTC trough to a 15–21:00 UTC peak — i.e. it's busiest during Americas daytime and European evening, and quietest during Asia's daytime. So the human side of this market looks Western-skewed, not Asia-centric. The contrast with the AMM is the tell: its fills are far flatter across the day (a ~1.5× swing vs the humans' ~2×), because a bot quoting a pool doesn't sleep. Humans keep office hours; the machines don't.

Part IIIHow the market behaves

Two speeds: how long offers take to fill

The bots-versus-humans split is visible in the single most basic mechanic: how long an offer takes to fill.

That speed split decides what it's like to provide liquidity. If you were a market maker weighing whether to deploy capital on Chia, the liquid XCH↔USD market is encouraging on price and discouraging on everything else. The catch is what happens when you rest a quote. Sort settled offers by how far they were priced from fair, and the time-to-fill collapses on the cheap side: an offer priced a bargain for the taker (>5% below fair) fills in a median of ~38 seconds (65% sub-minute), while a fairly-priced one sits for ~10 minutes. The picker is the AMM — TibetSwap fills in a median 51s vs ~18 minutes for everyone else. A resting offer is, in effect, a free option you write to arbitrage bots: they exercise it the instant it moves in their favor.

So the honest verdict for a would-be market maker: spreads are tight, but depth is thin and stale quotes get picked off in seconds. And there's a hard ceiling on size: the largest single fill in four years was 2,582 XCH, the 99th-percentile trade is just ~24 XCH (the median 0.2), and the busiest liquid pair clears only ~$800/day — so any real position has to be sliced across many fills and many days. You can't passively rest orders and collect a spread here — you must quote actively and reprice with the market, or the bots will pick you off on every move. (A caveat worth repeating: this is settled trades only, so the pick-off is inferred from how fast favorable offers fill, not from watching quotes get pulled.)

… but you're paid to do it

That verdict has a crucial other half: dexie pays market makers to keep quoting. Its Liquidity Incentive Program hands out DBX (dexie's token) to offers that stay within 5% of the market price on a handful of pairs — an estimated 16–31% APR on the liquid stablecoin pairs (and 100–340% on thin ones) — and only open offers earn. So the constant repricing that gets you picked off is exactly the behavior being subsidized. The payout is steady — ~24,000 DBX a month — but here's a twist you only see by pricing it through the order flow: valued in dollars (DBX→XCH→USD from our own reconstructed prices), the subsidy shrank ~5×, from about $1,700/month in mid-2025 to ~$355 by 2026 (~$8,600 over the 10 months we can see), because the reward is fixed in a token whose value fell with XCH. And the response is a frenzy of quoting: 2.2 million offers earned rewards in just 10 months, of which 99.7% never settled — quotes re-posted to track the market, not trades. That churn is a big reason so many offer files exist at all (and why the lifecycle counts earlier undercount it).

This is the churn the lifecycle counts couldn't see

Earlier, dexie's per-status counts seemed to say settled offers were a big slice of the total. The reward data exposes why that's wrong: those counts are pruned. Sample old reward-earning offer ids and dexie no longer has them (the API returns "not found"); the recent ones it does keep are all cancelled or expired, never settled. So reward offers are essentially a census of market-maker churn — and it's enormous: 150,000–280,000 reward-earning offers a month, versus 17,000–39,000 that settle.

That's a ratio of roughly 8–9 churned offers for every settled one, and it's reasonably steady month to month. Tempting as it is to use that ratio to back out cancellations for the earlier years (where dexie pruned them), we shouldn't: this churn is a creature of the incentive program, which was smaller or absent before 2025, so the ratio almost certainly wasn't ~8× in 2022–23. What the reward data does give is a hard floor: incentivized makers alone churned 2.2 million offers in 10 months, so cumulative cancellations and expirations run into the millions — orders of magnitude above the ~91k + 228k the pruned status counts show.

So — is making markets here worth it?

Putting it together: it has been a rewards game, not a spread game. The spreads are real but the venue is tiny (~$800/day on the liquid pair, trades capped around the low tens of XCH), so you don't earn a living capturing spread — and you're picked off the moment your quote is stale. What made it pay was the DBX subsidy (16–31% APR on the stablecoin pairs), which is why a few dozen pros do it at scale (the top 100 addresses take 86% of rewards) and churn millions of quotes. But the catch for anyone considering it now: that subsidy is paid in a falling token and has already shrunk ~5× ($1,700→$355/month), so the easy yield is largely gone — what's left is a thin, bot-dominated market where you must quote actively, against incumbents who already own most of the rewards.

The plumbing: fees, cost, and junk

Fees: 81.4% of trades pay nothing. The ~19% who pay are almost all fast fungible swaps — 42% of sub-minute fills pay a fee versus 0.7% of offers that sat more than a month. On Chia a fee is a priority bid for block inclusion, paid only when bots race during busy periods; a patient NFT listing has no race to win, so it rides for free. (This is the on-chain network fee. It's separate from any service fee — dexie's Combined Swap, for instance, takes 1% of the XCH — which isn't in this field, so "81% pay nothing" is about network fees, not the cost of using an aggregator.)

That parenthetical deserves a chart, because it flips the usual intuition. An offer can pay three fees — the blockchain network fee, the ~1% aggregator fee on a Combined Swap, and (for an NFT) the creator royalty — and the one everyone fixates on is the smallest by a wide margin. Across the whole dataset, network fees total only ~130 XCH, versus an estimated ~2,860 XCH in aggregator fees and ~9,745 XCH in NFT royalties. The chain takes a rounding error; the real cost of trading is the service fee and the royalty.

Cost: an NFT trade is ~15% cheaper to settle than a token swap (the token's puzzle layer is the real cost, not the NFT), and the fee paid is essentially uncorrelated with a trade's actual compute cost (≈0.06) — it's a tip for speed, not a price for space.

One framing caveat — read the next chart carefully. It measures offers' share of Chia's theoretical compute capacity: their CLVM execution cost against the per-block cost limit. That number is tiny (~0.5%) because offer settlements are deliberately cheap to verify — which is a strength, not a sign of irrelevance: a whole decentralized exchange runs on ~half a percent of the chain's compute headroom. It is emphatically not offers' share of actual block usage. Chia blocks normally run far below their limit, so against the compute that actually runs, offer settlements are a much larger slice — which the next chart finally measures. So: cheap to compute, but a load-bearing primitive. (Mechanics: expired offers never touch the chain; on-chain cancellations do, batched — the orange band adds them at ~0.2× the settled cost, peak ~0.53% → ~0.64%.)

That capacity figure answers “could the chain handle this?” — but not the question most people mean: how much of what actually runs on Chia is offer files? To get that we sampled the real CLVM cost of thousands of blocks (via a full-node API) and compared offers' compute to the chain's actual total. It's far larger: offers were ~6% of all Chia compute through 2024, then jumped to ~35–40% in 2025–26 — about 10% all-time.

It's worth sanity-checking that footprint against raw throughput, and the two agree: even in its busiest month the entire settled offer-file market cleared about one trade every 69 seconds — roughly one offer every four Chia blocks. A market that fills ~1 order a minute can't help but be a featherweight on a chain producing a block every ~19 seconds.

Junk: the bad prices you'd worry about barely exist in settled data — anyone can post a ridiculous price, but someone must accept it, and under 0.5% of settled trades are 10× outliers (a rate that's falling). The format also migrated in a hard cliff: version 2 went from 0% to 89% of offers between January and March 2023.

A price oracle hiding in the order flow

Here is one genuinely useful thing the dataset gives you for free. Because every settled offer is a real, accepted price, the order flow is secretly a price oracle. Using only XCH-against-stablecoin trades — no external feed — you can reconstruct XCH's dollar price. To check that claim, the chart below overlays the reconstruction on the true market price from an outside exchange feed (CryptoCompare): the two lines sit almost on top of each other, within a few percent week to week.

One practical caveat, the depegged-stablecoin trap from Part I: build this on USDSC instead of the warp.green coins and Chia looks like a $160–$695 coin, because a depegged "dollar" is worth far less than a dollar. The fix is just coin selection — validate a peg against the data before you trust it as a yardstick.

How much money actually moved

The oracle trick generalizes: if offers can price XCH, they can price anything. Take XCH as the unit, value each XCH↔asset trade at the true XCH price that day, derive every other token's XCH price from its own XCH trades, and you can put a dollar figure on 96.6% of all 833k settled offers. (A swap is atomic, so its two sides are equal in value — you only need to price one; liquidity add/remove on TibetSwap is excluded, since that isn't a trade.) Summed over four years, the offer-file system has settled about $16.9 million — and because the data is settled-only and undercounts the oldest trades of the busiest pairs, read that as a floor.

But $16.9M spread across 805k priced swaps averages ~$21 a swap, and the average lies. The typical swap is tiny: roughly 76% are worth under a dollar and the median is ~$2. More telling is how that moved over time. Plotting the whole monthly distribution — not one line but the full spread — the median swap collapsed from ~$34 in 2022 to under a dollar by 2026, for two compounding reasons: XCH itself fell ~15×, and the mix flooded with sub-cent game-economy token transfers that barely existed early on. The spread fanned open with it — a single recent month now runs from fractions of a cent to ~$100.

What offer files became

Offer files were designed as a trustless, peer-to-peer swap primitive. The data shows what people made of that primitive, which is something messier and more alive: a retail bazaar, built first on NFTs, increasingly run by trading bots, doubling as a price oracle, and quietly hosting entire game economies — all run by a small cast of humans and bots, and, in a nice inversion of the usual worry, one where trustlessness protects NFT creators' royalties instead of cheating them. The mechanism was built for one thing; people used it for everything. That gap — between what a protocol is for and what it becomes — is the whole reason the receipts are worth reading.
